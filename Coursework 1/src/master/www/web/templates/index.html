About

HeadAid was founded by a group of undergraduate engineers from the Electrical and Electronic Engineering Department at Imperial College London. The project was proposed when co-founder Alex Luisi, an avid ice hockey player and member of the university team, realised how common concussions are within ice hockey and how easy it is to miss the signs of Traumatic Brain Injury (TBI), especially when players feel uninjured and want to return to the ice. The HeadAid project aims to provide a way to pinpoint when a concussion may have been sustained through IoT technology and machine learning methods to ultimately improve the safety and wellbeing within the sport and beyond.

Functionality

With concussions being reported one of the most frequent injuries in Ice Hockey, and renewed efforts to better understand Traumatic Brain Injuries (TBI), there is demand to enhance identification of a potential injury. Studies have shown that NHL players experience an average of 1.8 concussions every 1000 hours of play and almost double that for juniors, with 12% of each year reporting a head injury and that of these head injuries that are sustained, over half may go unreported demonstrating that concussions are a regular occurrence across all leagues. In fact, mounting lawsuits and pressure on the league has bolstered efforts to overhaul traditional diagnosis and treatment methodology in the ice hockey community as a whole. Research has also demonstrated that concussions can be sustained without direct contact to the head as a result of checking. For frequent players, these recurrent injuries can lead to significant problems later on in life, such as permanent memory loss and changes in behaviour. Although no helmet will provide complete protection from injury, the HeadAid sensor system provides a real-time, connected solution for immediate impact identification which could result in reducing subsequent and potentially more serious damage during a match.

The main indicators that lead to an accurate diagnosis lie with detecting acceleration and angular velocity relating to head movement. The HeadAid system, which combines both an accelerometer and gyroscope to record these accelerations, is discreetly nestled between the airflow gaps in the helmet padding to prevent it from external damage and provide more accurate data capture, by bringing it closer to the head. Machine Learning techniques are used to give detailed thresholds and identify trends to pinpoint the impacts most likely to cause TBI and determine whether a player should be examined. Inherent cloud-integration to provides a web application, scalable from desktop to mobile, to empower a coach and team doctor to monitor all of their team from the bench in real time
